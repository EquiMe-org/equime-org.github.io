We founded equiMe almost a year ago. Here's what I didn't expect.

## Starting Was the Hardest Part

Looking back, it should have been the easiest. Once you start, you iterate. You plan, you strategize, and execution becomes almost mechanical. You just do the next thing. But getting started? Being comfortable not knowing what the end looks like? That took courage I didn't expect to need.

The anxiety wasn't about failure. It was about ambiguity. I didn't know what I didn't know, and the landscape of things I needed to learn felt infinite. The only way through it was to start anyway and figure it out on the move.

## The Double Life

Here's the part nobody warns you about: building a company while holding down a full-time job.

I adjusted my work hours to 6am to 3pm. I take an hour to reset. Then I work on equiMe from 4pm to midnight, most days of the week. Whatever doesn't get done in the week gets done on the weekend.

It's cost me. Physical health has taken a back seat. Social life is essentially nonexistent. Things like cooking dinner feel like luxuries because they take too much time. Every hour has to count, and right now most of them go to building.

Last year I ran a marathon with my cofounder. That wasn't about fitness. It was a test. How far can you keep going after you've already given in to the negative thoughts? How long can you endure after you're already tired? The marathon was a metaphor we could measure in miles. We finished.

## AI Changed Everything (and Made Everything Harder)

One of the biggest surprises was how much AI accelerated our ability to build. We can ship significantly faster, get recommendations, and research things that would have taken 10x the time a few years ago. For a tiny team with no funding, that's the difference between existing and not.

The flip side is real though. The AI landscape is advancing so fast that keeping up is a part-time job on its own. New models, new tools, new capabilities every week. And that's before you factor in the policy changes constantly being passed that affect how we build and what we can offer.

The pace of change is both our greatest tailwind and our most relentless headwind.

## The Puffin

Early on I made a list of topics I needed to go deeper on: economics, crypto, leadership, AI, legal, VC, accounting, finance, raising strategy, statistics. I'm not starting from zero on any of these. But I'm also not a career finance person or an AI subject matter expert. These are deep domains and building a company that touches all of them means you're always learning.

The biggest thing I got wrong early was thinking I needed to have all the answers. I don't. What I need is to know who to turn to when I need the right questions answered.

My favorite animal is the puffin. Nobody makes movies about puffins. They're not apex predators. They're not majestic. But puffins are adaptable. They swim, they fly, they walk on land. They live in some of the harshest environments on earth and they thrive.

I want to be a puffin. Adaptable to the times. Flexible in harsh environments. Not the biggest or the strongest, but built to survive and keep going.

Leadership isn't about knowing everything. It's about being adaptable and knowing where to find the answers.

## Culture Is Deliberate

The other thing I learned this year: culture doesn't happen by accident. It's established deliberately, through every decision and every interaction.

At first I worked with a bit of reservedness. Careful, measured, maybe too cautious. I don't do that anymore. We're trying to solve a problem that could help billions of people. I need to fully internalize that, so when I go to work, people see the motivation in my words and my actions.

Set the example. Work the hardest. Care the most. Not because I expect anyone to match my hours, but because the standard you walk past is the standard you accept.

---

A year of building equiMe taught me more than any job I've ever held. Not about technology or markets. About myself. About what I'm willing to sacrifice, what I refuse to compromise on, and what kind of leader I want to be when this thing takes off.

Because it will.
